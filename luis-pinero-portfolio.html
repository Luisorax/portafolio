I'm Luis, a travel documentary photographer and cinematographer working across still and moving image.

My work is drawn to places where landscape, culture, and human experience remain deeply connected — remote territories, fragile traditions, rituals, and the quiet structures of everyday life far from the familiar.

I'm interested in images that do more than describe. I look for atmosphere, tension, intimacy, and the relationship between people and place. Not just what something looks like, but what it feels like to be there.

Alongside commissioned and personal work, I develop expedition-based projects through Tierra Ignota — a platform focused on immersive journeys into culturally and geographically significant environments. That experience has shaped how I work: with patience, adaptability, and a strong awareness that trust matters as much as vision.

Whether working alone or in collaboration, my approach remains the same — attentive, grounded, and committed to building images with depth, not just surface.

South Sudan — 2025  ·  Documentary photography

Documentary Photography

Mundari
Cattle Camps

South Sudan  ·  2025

↳ Documentary photography  ·  Personal project

A visual exploration of life within the Mundari cattle camps, where daily existence is shaped by cattle, smoke, and ritual.

The project focuses on the relationship between humans and livestock as a central structure of identity, economy, and presence. Visually, the work leans into atmosphere — ash, light diffusion, density — avoiding distance in favour of immersion. Access required working within a delicate cultural context, where presence needed to be negotiated with respect and time. The result is a body of work that moves between documentary and sensory experience, rather than pure observation.

03 — Field Experience Documentary · Expedition · Cultural Immersion Remote & Culturally Complex Environments
03 — Field Experience

In the field,
not from a distance.

My work is closely tied to field experience. Over the years, I have worked across remote and culturally complex environments — often in conditions that require flexibility, patience, and sensitivity, not just technical execution.

From isolated landscapes to communities with strong traditions and identities, these experiences shape both how I approach people and how I build images.

Through Tierra Ignota, I also develop and lead small-group expeditions designed around cultural immersion and meaningful encounters. This dual perspective — as both image-maker and expedition lead — informs my work with a deeper understanding of place, logistics, and human context.

I don't approach travel as spectacle, but as a space for attention, respect, and long-form observation.

i.

East Africa & The Horn

Remote Territories,
Living Cultures

South Sudan, Ethiopia, Djibouti. Work made in communities shaped by ritual, cattle, and a sense of place that precedes the photograph. Access built through time, not permits.

ii.

Central Asia & The Steppe

Nomadic Routes
& Seasonal Migration

Mongolia, Kyrgyzstan, the Wakhan Corridor. Landscapes without roads, communities organized around movement. Work that required months of preparation and the willingness to return.

iii.

The Himalaya & Karakoram

High Altitude
& Mountain Cultures

Pakistan, Nepal, Ladakh. Expeditions above 4,000 metres, in regions where permit processes, altitude, and cultural protocol are part of the work — not obstacles to it.

iv.

The Arabian Peninsula

Desert Territory
& Ancient Trade

Oman, UAE. Work shaped by hospitality culture, the specific visual demands of extreme heat, and the patience required to read a landscape that reveals itself slowly.

v.

The Andes & Amazonia

Fragile Traditions
& Living Ritual

Peru, Bolivia, Ecuador. High-altitude portrait work, Andean ceremonial communities, Amazonian territory documentation carried out with community consent and respect at every stage.

vi.

Southern Europe & North Africa

Margins, Memory
& Transition

Morocco, Algeria, Southern Spain, Sardinia. Shorter-form editorial and documentary commissions. The visual grammar of border zones — where cultures remember each other.

Way of Looking

Not interested in reducing
travel to spectacle.

The most meaningful images rarely come from distance alone, but from the quality of attention brought to a place.

For me, documentary work begins before the camera — in listening, in waiting, in understanding rhythm, in recognizing when to step closer and when not to.

I'm drawn to images that hold both beauty and complexity. Images that don't simplify what they show, but allow space for ambiguity, atmosphere, and presence.

What matters is not only what is seen, but how it is approached.
